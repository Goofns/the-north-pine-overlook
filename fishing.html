Fly Fishing Near Bailey, Colorado

Wild trout on the South Platte, a stocked alpine lake, and easy water for first-timers, all within reach of the cabin above Platte Canyon.

The North Pine Overlook  /  Fly Fishing Near Bailey

The South Platte is one of Colorado's great trout rivers, and you are staying right above it. Whether you are a practiced fly angler or teaching a kid to cast for the first time, there is water close to the cabin to match. Here is where to fish near Bailey.

The North Fork of the South Platte

The North Fork winds through Platte Canyon just below town, holding wild brown and rainbow trout in pockets, riffles, and runs. It is approachable, wadeable water that rewards a careful approach and a light touch, which makes it a great place to spend a relaxed morning. Look for public access points along the canyon, and respect private stretches and posted land.

Wellington Lake

For a near-guaranteed catch and a beautiful setting, Wellington Lake sits about forty minutes southeast, a private, stocked alpine lake ringed by peaks and pines. It is regularly stocked with several kinds of trout and is calm, family-friendly water, ideal when you want everyone to land a fish. There is a use fee, so check current rates before you go.

Easy water for beginners

  • The Davis Ponds at Staunton State Park are calm and accessible, a low-pressure spot to practice casting on a half day of hiking.
  • Stocked pond operations in the Pine area, such as Rainbow Trout Ranch, are built for teaching kids to catch their first trout.

A bigger day on the Platte

If you want to chase the river's reputation, the famous tailwater stretches of the South Platte downstream (around Deckers and the canyons) draw fly anglers from all over for their wild trout and technical water. It is a worthwhile day trip for a serious angler staying at the cabin.

Before you go

Anyone 16 and older needs a Colorado fishing license, which you can buy online in a few minutes from Colorado Parks and Wildlife or at local shops. Check current regulations for each stretch of water, since some are catch-and-release or fly-and-lure only. Bring layers, polarized sunglasses, and plenty of water, and practice good catch-and-release to keep the fishery healthy for the next visit.

The Best Fly Fishing Trips on the South Platte River, Colorado

Wild trout on the North Fork below Bailey, the technical water of Cheesman Canyon, and easy access at Deckers, all from a cabin an hour from Denver.

The North Pine Overlook  /  South Platte Fly Fishing Trips

The South Platte is one of the great trout rivers of the American West, and the cabin sits right above one of its most overlooked stretches. There is enough fishable water in the South Platte drainage to keep an angler busy for years, from small pocket water you can wade in an afternoon to Gold Medal canyons that humble people who have fished their whole lives. This is a guide to the best fly fishing trips on the river, starting with the water closest to your door in Bailey and working out to the famous stretches downstream.

The North Fork below Bailey, the one everyone drives past

The North Fork of the South Platte runs through Platte Canyon just below Bailey, and it is the reason a fishing trip here is different from a fishing trip anywhere else on the river. It is a smaller stream of pocket water, riffles, and runs, holding wild brown and rainbow trout that see far less pressure than the crowds downstream. Most anglers drive right past it on the way to Cheesman or Deckers, which is exactly why it fishes so well. On a summer weekday you can have a stretch of canyon nearly to yourself while Deckers is shoulder to shoulder, and the summer dry-fly fishing to eager browns is the kind of relaxed morning that makes a trip. Look for public access points along the canyon, watch for posted private land, and fish it light and careful. From the cabin it is a matter of minutes, not a drive.

Cheesman Canyon, the graduate school of the South Platte

If the North Fork is the friendly water out the door, Cheesman Canyon is the test you drive to. This three-mile stretch below Cheesman Reservoir is designated Gold Medal water by Colorado Parks and Wildlife, meaning it holds an exceptional density of trout, and it runs through a granite canyon studded with ponderosa pines that is arguably the most beautiful stretch on the whole river. The fish here have seen more flies than almost any trout in the state. Getting in requires a hike, which keeps the pressure down, and once you are there the reward is crystal-clear water, technical hatches, and browns and rainbows that demand a perfect drift. The saying on this river is simple: if you can fool a Cheesman trout on a tiny midge, you can catch fish anywhere. Come humble, fish small, and treat it as catch and release.

Deckers, the forgiving one

Downstream of Cheesman, the Deckers stretch is where a lot of people learn to fly fish on the South Platte, and for good reason. Almost all of the access is roadside with ample parking, the river is easy to wade, and the trout population is healthy and willing. Brown trout dominate the caddis, mayfly, and stonefly water here, with more rainbows showing up every year. It lends itself to beginners and first-time anglers in a way the canyons do not, which makes it a good choice for a mixed group where not everyone is chasing the hardest fish on the river. Winter fishing below the canyon can be excellent too, when the tailwater stays cold and the crowds thin out.

Worth the drive: the Dream Stream and Eleven Mile Canyon

For a bigger day, the upper river down in South Park is worth the drive. The Dream Stream, the open stretch between Spinney Mountain and Eleven Mile reservoirs, is famous trophy water where large rainbows and browns run up from the reservoirs in the spring and fall migrations. Nearby Eleven Mile Canyon offers clear, cold tailwater with deep pools and structured runs made for nymphing and dry flies. Both are a longer haul from Bailey than the canyon water, so they suit a dedicated full-day trip rather than a quick morning, but for a serious angler they belong on the list.

When to go, season by season

The South Platte fishes all year, and the season shapes the trip. Winter is quiet and productive on the lower tailwaters below Cheesman, with midges and fewer people. Spring brings the pre-runoff window and the first migrations onto the Dream Stream. Summer is the North Fork's time, long mild days and eager dry-fly browns close to the cabin, with cool nights to come home to. Fall is the other trophy season, with browns running and the canyon walls set against gold aspen. Because most of the river is tailwater, hatches are reliable across the whole year, which is part of what makes it such a dependable fishery.

Flies and tactics that work here

The South Platte is a technical river, so small and precise beats big and flashy. Midges in the smallest sizes carry the winter and much of the year, tiny mayfly patterns for the baetis, and caddis and stoneflies in their seasons. On the North Fork in summer you can move fish on dries, which is the most fun way to fish it. Everywhere on this river the presentation matters more than the pattern, so fish a light tippet, mend for a clean drift, and work the seams and pockets rather than the flat open water. When in doubt, go a size smaller.

Before you go

Anyone 16 and older needs a Colorado fishing license, which takes a few minutes to buy online from Colorado Parks and Wildlife at cpwshop.com. For 2026 a resident annual runs about $45 and a nonresident annual about $124, but if you are visiting for a day or two the nonresident one-day (about $22) or five-day (about $41) is the better value, and a Habitat Stamp is required for adults. Regulations change by stretch, and Gold Medal water like Cheesman Canyon is catch-and-release, so check the posted signs and the current CPW rules for the exact water you fish. Bring layers, polarized sunglasses, and plenty of water, and practice good catch and release to keep the fishery healthy for the next visit.

A basecamp on the water

What ties all of this together is where you stay. The North Pine Overlook sits in the pines above Bailey, minutes from the North Fork and roughly an hour from Denver, which puts the quiet home water out the door and the famous canyon water within an easy drive. Sleeps six, with room to dry your waders and a deck to sit on when the fishing is done. The cabin opens in 2026, and fall aspen dates, prime brown-trout season, open for booking in late August. If you are planning a South Platte trip, this is a good place to run it from.

Midsummer on the North Fork below Bailey. Warm, mild days and cool nights, with low-and-clear water once the early-season runoff settles. Mornings and evenings fish best in the heat, and terrestrials start to matter as the summer goes on. Down in Cheesman Canyon the tailwater stays cold and technical through the warm months. Always check current flows and any fire restrictions before you go.

A Cabin Near Staunton State Park, Colorado

Elk Falls, thirty miles of trails, calm ponds for a first cast, and granite for climbers, all about fifteen minutes from the cabin.

The North Pine Overlook  /  Staunton State Park

If you ask us for one day trip from the cabin, this is it. Staunton State Park sits about fifteen minutes up US 285, nearly four thousand acres that climb from grassy meadows around 8,100 feet to granite domes above 10,000, with a waterfall tucked in the middle. It is the kind of park people drive out from Denver for; you will be there before your coffee cools.

Why Staunton is the one to see

The park packs an unusual amount of Colorado into one place: open meadows, aspen and conifer forest, rock formations that glow in the evening, and long views back across the foothills. Thirty miles of trails link it all together, shared by hikers, mountain bikers, and horseback riders, and the network is laid out so you can make a mellow morning or a full day of it. Wildflowers are famously good here in early and mid summer.

The hike to do: Elk Falls

The signature outing is the trek to Elk Falls, a roughly 11-mile round trip that most visitors break up with a pause at the Elk Falls overlook. It is a solid half-day with real elevation gain, so start early, carry water, and treat it as the main event. If you want the postcard with less mileage, the Staunton Ranch Trail wanders through meadows and pines from the main parking areas and turns around wherever you like.

Easy wins with kids

The Davis Ponds loop is the family move: a short, gentle walk to two calm ponds stocked with trout, with an ADA-accessible fishing pier. It pairs perfectly with a picnic, and it is the same water we point beginner anglers to on our fishing page. Anyone 16 or older needs a Colorado fishing license, which takes a few minutes to buy online.

Climbing, archery, and the Track-Chair

Climbers get more than 400 routes on the park's granite, from friendly slabs to hard test pieces, with some areas closed in spring and early summer to protect nesting raptors. There is also a 17-lane archery range, and Staunton runs a Track-Chair program that puts all-terrain motorized chairs on designated trails, one of the few parks in the state where visitors with mobility challenges can get properly into the backcountry.

Getting there from the cabin

Head north on US 285 to Shaffers Crossing, about six miles west of Conifer, then follow Elk Creek Road a mile and a half to the entrance. From the cabin door to the gate is around fifteen minutes, which means you can be on trail at eight and back in the hot tub by two.

Before you go

Staunton charges a daily vehicle fee (about $10, or free with a Colorado state parks pass), and the closest lot, Lazy V, fills early on summer and fall weekends, so arrive in the morning. The visitor center opens at 8 a.m. daily. Dogs are welcome on leash. In September the aspen pockets inside the park turn gold, and weekday mornings are the quiet way to see them.
